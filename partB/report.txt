Paper: Revisiting k-means: New Algorithms via Bayesian Nonparametrics
Authors: Brian Kulis & Michael I. Jordan | Venue: ICML 2012

1. Paper Summary
Kulis and Jordan (2012) observe that standard k-means requires the practitioner to specify the
number of clusters k before the algorithm runs, with no principled built-in mechanism for choosing
k from data. Their key insight is that k-means can be re-derived as the small-variance asymptotic
limit (σ2→0) of the Expectation-Maximisation (EM) algorithm applied to a finite Gaussian Mixture
Model (GMM). They extend this derivation to the Dirichlet Process Gaussian Mixture Model
(DP-GMM) — a nonparametric Bayesian model that places a prior over the number of mixture
components — and show that taking σ2→0 in the DP-GMM Gibbs sampler yields a new
hard-assignment algorithm they call DP-means. DP-means is structurally identical to k-means but
replaces the fixed-k requirement with a scalar penalty parameter λ (lambda): whenever a point's
squared Euclidean distance to all current cluster centers exceeds λ, a new cluster is created
automatically. The algorithm provably converges to a local minimum of a penalised within-cluster
sum-of-squares objective (Eq. 9), and the authors demonstrate it on synthetic data, image
patches, and speaker segmentation tasks, showing it matches or outperforms
model-selection-based approaches for choosing k.

2. Reproduction Setup and Result
Dataset: A 2D synthetic dataset generated with sklearn.datasets.make_blobs (300 samples, 4
Gaussian blobs, cluster_std=0.8, random_state=42). This dataset was chosen because it directly
satisfies DP-means' core assumption of spherical isotropic Gaussian clusters (Section 4, Eq. 9),
allowing clean verification of whether the algorithm recovers k=4 without k being specified.
Implementation: DP-means was implemented from scratch in Python following Algorithm 1 in the
paper. The hyperparameter λ=15 was chosen by scanning λ ∈ {1.5, 3, 5, 8, 10, 15, 20, 30, 50} and
selecting the smallest value that consistently recovers k=4. The evaluation metric is inertia — the
within-cluster sum of squared Euclidean distances (Eq. 9), also used by k-means.

Commentary on results: DP-means achieves exactly the same inertia as k-means with the
oracle k=4 (362.47), while discovering k automatically from data. This is consistent with the

paper's theoretical argument (Section 3): on well-separated spherical Gaussian clusters,
DP-means converges to the same local minimum as k-means. No gap appears on this idealized
dataset. In the paper's real-world experiments (Section 5), DP-means must also implicitly perform
model selection for k — the advantage there is that λ provides a single, interpretable parameter
rather than requiring multiple k-means runs with a selection criterion. The toy dataset cannot
reveal this advantage, since k=4 is trivially correct.

3. Ablation Study Findings
Ablation 1 — Removing New-Cluster Creation (λ = ∞)
Setting λ=∞ disables the new-cluster creation rule (Algorithm 1, lines 5–6) — the mechanism by
which DP-means discovers k from data. The result is k=1 and inertia=19,780: a 54× increase over
the full method. All 300 points are forced into a single cluster centered at their global mean. This
ablation confirms that the λ-threshold is the entire operational contribution of DP-means over
k-means. Without it, the algorithm degenerates to a trivial mean estimator, losing its nonparametric
character completely. From a Bayesian perspective, λ=∞ corresponds to placing all prior
probability on a single mixture component — removing the Dirichlet Process prior that enables
flexible k discovery.

Ablation 2 — Removing Iterative Center Re-estimation (Single-Pass)
Running only one sweep of the assignment step without updating cluster centers afterwards
(ablating Algorithm 1, line 10) yields k=8 and inertia=1,847 — a 5× increase over the full method.
Without iterative updates, centers remain at their first-assigned positions (sequential points in the
dataset) rather than converging to cluster means. Points encountered later in the sweep encounter
poorly positioned centers, causing excessive new-cluster creation (distance exceeds λ not
because a genuinely new cluster exists, but because the center is displaced). This demonstrates
that the iterative M-step update (Algorithm 1, line 10) is essential for both accurate inertia
minimisation and correct k recovery — it is not a cosmetic refinement but a structural requirement
for convergence.
Joint interpretation: Together the two ablations isolate the two components of DP-means that
make it work: (1) the λ-threshold for automatic k discovery, and (2) iterative center refinement for
accuracy. Removing either one independently causes substantial degradation, confirming both are
load-bearing parts of the algorithm.

4. Failure Mode and Honest Reflection
Failure Mode: Non-Spherical (Moon-Shaped) Clusters
DP-means was tested on sklearn.datasets.make_moons (300 samples, noise=0.1) — two
interleaving crescent-shaped clusters. This directly violates Assumption 1 (Task 1.2): DP-means
uses squared Euclidean distance (Section 4, Eq. 9), which implicitly assumes all clusters are
isotropic Gaussians. Euclidean Voronoi partitions produce straight-line boundaries that cannot
capture curved manifold structure.
At no value of λ does DP-means correctly recover the two crescents. Small λ (0.05–0.3) fragments
each moon into 8–30 spherical sub-clusters; large λ (1.0–2.0) finds 2–3 blobs that cut across the
moon boundaries incorrectly. The failure is fundamental — it cannot be resolved by tuning λ
because the problem is the distance metric, not the threshold. The small-variance asymptotic
derivation (Theorem 1) compounds this by collapsing soft posteriors to hard assignments,

removing the boundary ambiguity that could partially mitigate the spherical assumption. A concrete
fix would be to replace Euclidean distance with a kernel distance (e.g., RBF kernel), yielding a
kernelised DP-means that operates in a feature space where the moons become linearly
separable.

Honest Reflection
What could not be implemented: The paper's original experiments use real-world datasets —
image patch clustering (Section 5.2) and speaker diarisation (Section 5.3). These were not
reproduced because they require large proprietary or licensed datasets and preprocessing
pipelines far beyond the scope of this submission. The comparison against full DP-GMM inference
(which requires MCMC) was also omitted for compute reasons.
What was surprising: The sensitivity of DP-means to λ was larger than expected — on the
make_blobs dataset, λ values between 10 and 50 all give k=4, but below 10 the algorithm rapidly
over-clusters. This suggests that while λ is more interpretable than k, it still requires careful tuning
and is not a free parameter in practice. The identical inertia between DP-means (λ=15) and
k-means (k=4) on this clean dataset was also surprising — it confirms the theoretical equivalence
but leaves no empirical daylight between the methods on ideal data.
What would be revisited with more time: The kernelised DP-means variant hinted at in the
paper (and suggested as a failure-mode fix) would be the first priority. Additionally, a sensitivity
analysis of λ on a noisier, higher-dimensional dataset would better stress-test the algorithm.
Finally, a timing comparison between DP-means and k-means + BIC/AIC model selection would
quantify the computational advantage the paper claims.
